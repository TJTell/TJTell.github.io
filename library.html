The Library

My interests often extend beyond the technical requirements of accounting. Take a glimpse into my personal library to see some of the books I recommend—ranging from narrative-driven fiction to investigative non-fiction and academic study.

Fantasy

Gardens of the Moon

Malazan Books of the Fallen

by Steven Erikson

Set against the backdrop of a sprawling empire’s campaign for global conquest, the story follows the Bridgeburners—an elite but battered unit of soldiers—as they navigate a siege that is quickly spiraling out of control. It serves as an introduction to a world with hundreds of thousands of years of history, where the machinations of gods and ancient powers frequently collide with—and often upend—the lives of everyone caught in their wake.

Fantasy

The Black Company

The Chronicles of the Black Company

by Glen Cook

Following a disastrous contract, the Black Company—the last of the mercenary Free Companies of Khatovar—is hired by a mysterious soul-catcher to join a war against the fanatical White Rose rebels. The story is told through the journals of Croaker, the Company’s physician and annalist, as he chronicles what may be the deadliest, and perhaps final, assignment in their long history.

Sci-Fi

The Martian

by Andy Weir

During a manned mission to Mars, astronaut Mark Watney is presumed dead and left behind after a fierce storm forces his crew to evacuate. Armed only with his ingenuity, botanical expertise, and a refusal to quit, Watney must leverage his limited resources and technical knowledge to survive on a hostile planet. It is a compelling study of human resilience and the practical application of the scientific method under extreme logistical constraints.

Non-Fiction

Moneyball

by Michael Lewis

In 2002, the Oakland Athletics faced a systemic crisis: a limited budget and a roster stripped of its stars. Rather than following the expensive, intuition-based traditions of Major League Baseball, General Manager Billy Beane implemented the principles of Sabermetrics to find value where other teams saw none. By leveraging empirical data to identify market inefficiencies, the A's proved that a rigorous, analytical framework could overcome significant financial disadvantages and fundamentally change how an organization measures success.

Non-Fiction

Double Entry: How the Merchants of Venice Shaped the Modern World

by Jane Gleeson-White

"Jane Gleeson-White traces the evolution of information management from the earliest known human writing—clay tokens used for accounting thousands of years ago—to the sophisticated systems of the Renaissance. The narrative centers on the rise of double-entry bookkeeping among Venetian merchants and its eventual codification by the friar Luca Pacioli. By connecting ancient accounting practices to the birth of modern capitalism, the book reveals how the structured recording of debits and credits became the essential language of global trade and economic stability."
